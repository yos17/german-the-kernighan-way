# Chapter 1: First Words

*Introduce yourself. Start speaking from sentence one.*

---

## The First Conversation

Before grammar, before cases, before any of the machinery — you can have a real conversation in German right now.

```
Hallo! Ich bin Anna.        Hello! I am Anna.
Wie heißt du?               What's your name?
Ich heiße Tom.              My name is Tom.
Woher kommst du?            Where are you from?
Ich komme aus Berlin.       I come from Berlin.
Wie geht es dir?            How are you?
Gut, danke. Und dir?        Good, thanks. And you?
```

Seven sentences. That's a real exchange. Every piece of grammar in those sentences will be explained fully in later chapters. For now, just use them.

---

## Verbs: The Engine of the Sentence

German sentences always have a verb. The verb changes its ending based on who's doing the action. This is called **conjugation**.

Three verbs you need immediately:

### sein — to be

| Person | German | English |
|--------|--------|---------|
| I | ich **bin** | I am |
| you (informal) | du **bist** | you are |
| he/she/it | er/sie/es **ist** | he/she/it is |
| we | wir **sind** | we are |
| you (plural) | ihr **seid** | you are |
| they / you (formal) | sie/Sie **sind** | they are / you are |

```
Ich bin Arzt.           I am a doctor.
Du bist müde.           You are tired.
Er ist nett.            He is nice.
Wir sind hier.          We are here.
```

### heißen — to be named

```
Ich heiße Maria.        My name is Maria.
Er heißt Klaus.         His name is Klaus.
Wie heißt du?           What's your name?
```

`heiße`, `heißt`, `heißen` — the pattern is the same as other verbs. You'll see it soon.

### kommen — to come

```
Ich komme aus Frankfurt.    I come from Frankfurt.
Woher kommst du?            Where do you come from?
Sie kommt aus Japan.        She comes from Japan.
```

## The Two "You"s

German has two ways to say "you":

**du** — informal. Use with friends, family, children, peers.

**Sie** — formal. Use with strangers, professionals, anyone you'd call by their last name. Always capitalized.

```
Wie heißt du?       (to a friend)
Wie heißen Sie?     (to a professor, a stranger, your boss)
```

Start with `du`. You'll develop a feel for when `Sie` is appropriate. If in doubt in a formal context, use `Sie` — Germans won't be offended.

## Numbers 1–20

You need these immediately.

```
1  ein     11  elf
2  zwei    12  zwölf
3  drei    13  dreizehn
4  vier    14  vierzehn
5  fünf    15  fünfzehn
6  sechs   16  sechzehn
7  sieben  17  siebzehn
8  acht    18  achtzehn
9  neun    19  neunzehn
10 zehn    20  zwanzig
```

Notice the pattern 13–19: the unit + `zehn` (ten). `dreizehn` = three + ten. Logical.

Exceptions to notice: `sechzehn` drops the final `s` (not `sechszehn`), `siebzehn` drops the `en` (not `siebenzehn`). Just those two.

## Days, Months, Time

**Days:**
```
Montag      Monday
Dienstag    Tuesday
Mittwoch    Wednesday
Donnerstag  Thursday
Freitag     Friday
Samstag     Saturday
Sonntag     Sunday
```

Pattern: most days end in `-tag` (day). Mittwoch means "mid-week".

**Asking about time:**
```
Welcher Tag ist heute?      What day is today?
Heute ist Montag.           Today is Monday.
Wie viel Uhr ist es?        What time is it?
Es ist drei Uhr.            It is three o'clock.
Es ist halb vier.           It is half past three.  (literally: half four)
```

Note: `halb vier` means "half of four" = 3:30. This is different from English. Half past three in German is "halb vier" — you're counting toward the next hour, not from the last one.

## Sentence Structure: The Basic Pattern

German word order for simple statements is: **Subject → Verb → Rest**

```
Ich     bin     Anna.           I am Anna.
[subj]  [verb]  [rest]

Du      kommst  aus Berlin.     You come from Berlin.
[subj]  [verb]  [rest]
```

For now, this is all you need. In Chapter 5 we'll see how the word order flexes in more complex sentences.

---

## Questions: Two Types

**Yes/No questions**: the verb moves to the front.

```
Du bist müde.       You are tired.      (statement)
Bist du müde?       Are you tired?      (question)

Du kommst aus Berlin.   You come from Berlin.
Kommst du aus Berlin?   Do you come from Berlin?
```

**W-questions** (who, what, where, when, why): the question word comes first, then verb, then subject.

```
Woher kommst du?        Where do you come from?
Wie heißt du?           What's your name?
Was machst du?          What do you do? / What are you doing?
Wann kommst du?         When are you coming?
Warum bist du müde?     Why are you tired?
```

W-question words:
```
wer     who
was     what
wo      where
woher   where from
wohin   where to
wann    when
warum   why
wie     how
wie viel  how much
```

## Negation

To negate a verb, use **nicht** (not):

```
Ich bin müde.           I am tired.
Ich bin nicht müde.     I am not tired.

Er kommt aus Berlin.            He comes from Berlin.
Er kommt nicht aus Berlin.      He doesn't come from Berlin.
```

To negate a noun, use **kein** (not a / no):

```
Ich habe Zeit.          I have time.
Ich habe keine Zeit.    I have no time.
```

`kein` vs `nicht`:
- Use `kein` before a noun that would otherwise use `ein` (a/an)
- Use `nicht` for everything else

This distinction will become clearer in Chapter 2.

## Putting It Together: Introduce Yourself

Here's a full self-introduction you can use right now:

```
Hallo! Ich heiße [your name].
Ich komme aus [your country].
Ich bin [your age] Jahre alt.
Ich bin [your job].
Ich wohne in [your city].
Ich spreche ein bisschen Deutsch.
```

Translation:
```
Hello! My name is [name].
I come from [country].
I am [age] years old.
I am a [job].
I live in [city].
I speak a little German.
```

New words:
- `Jahre alt` — years old
- `wohnen` — to live (somewhere)
- `sprechen` — to speak
- `ein bisschen` — a little bit
