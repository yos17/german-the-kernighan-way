# Chapter 9: Modal Verbs

*können, müssen, dürfen, sollen, wollen, mögen — expressing ability, obligation, permission, and desire.*

---

## Modals Do One Thing

Modal verbs don't describe actions directly. They describe your **relationship to an action**:

- Can you do it? → `können`
- Must you do it? → `müssen`
- Are you allowed to? → `dürfen`
- Are you supposed to? → `sollen`
- Do you want to? → `wollen`
- Do you like/would you like to? → `mögen` / `möchten`

Each modal verb pairs with an infinitive that goes to the end of the clause. You learned the basics in Chapter 4. Here we go deeper — nuance, past tense, and the subtle differences between them.

---

## A Closer Look at Each Modal

### können — can, to be able to

Physical and mental ability. Learned skills. Possibility.

```
Ich kann schwimmen.             I can swim.
Kannst du mir helfen?           Can you help me?
Er kann sehr gut kochen.        He can cook very well.
Das kann nicht stimmen.         That can't be right.
Kann ich hier parken?           Can I park here?
```

Note the last one: `können` can also ask/give permission informally (like in English).

### müssen — must, to have to

Obligation, necessity. Internal compulsion or logical necessity.

```
Ich muss arbeiten.              I have to work.
Du musst mehr schlafen.         You have to sleep more.
Es muss stimmen.                It must be right. (logical conclusion)
Ich muss jetzt gehen.           I have to go now.
```

**Negative trap**: `nicht müssen` ≠ `must not`. It means `don't have to`.

```
Du musst nicht kommen.          You don't have to come.     (not "you must not come")
Du darfst nicht kommen.         You must not come.          (prohibition)
```

This is one of the biggest false-friend mistakes in German.

### dürfen — may, to be allowed to

Permission. Rules and social norms.

```
Darf ich fragen?                May I ask?
Hier darf man nicht rauchen.    You're not allowed to smoke here.
Kinder dürfen das nicht.        Children aren't allowed to do that.
Du darfst jetzt gehen.          You may go now.
```

`dürfen` is more formal/polite than `können` for asking permission.

### sollen — should, to be supposed to

Obligation from an external source — someone told you to, it's expected of you.

```
Du sollst nicht lügen.          You shall not lie. (commandment)
Er soll um acht Uhr kommen.     He's supposed to come at eight.
Was soll ich machen?            What am I supposed to do?
Sie soll sehr klug sein.        She is said to be very clever. (hearsay)
```

The last one is important: `soll` + `sein` = "is said to be" (reported speech from someone else).

```
Das Restaurant soll sehr gut sein.      The restaurant is supposed to be very good.
```

### wollen — to want to

Strong desire, intention, or will. More forceful than `möchten`.

```
Ich will Deutsch lernen.            I want to learn German.
Was willst du werden?               What do you want to be(come)?
Er will nicht kommen.               He doesn't want to come. / He refuses to come.
Wir wollen ein Haus kaufen.         We want to buy a house.
```

Note: `will` in German ≠ "will" in English (future). `Ich will` = "I want", not "I will".

### mögen — to like / möchten — would like

`mögen` expresses liking:

```
Ich mag Kaffee.             I like coffee.
Magst du Musik?             Do you like music?
Ich mag ihn nicht.          I don't like him.
```

`möchten` (subjunctive of mögen) is the polite "would like" — use this in shops, restaurants, requests:

```
Ich möchte einen Kaffee, bitte.     I would like a coffee, please.
Was möchten Sie trinken?            What would you like to drink?
Wir möchten einen Tisch reservieren. We would like to reserve a table.
```

`möchten` is the most polite way to make a request. Use it constantly.

## Modal + Infinitive: Word Order

In main clauses: modal in position 2, infinitive at the end.

```
Ich  kann  morgen  nicht  kommen.
[1]  [2]           [not]  [inf, end]
```

In subordinate clauses: infinitive before modal (both at the end).

```
Ich weiß, dass ich morgen nicht kommen kann.
I know that I can't come tomorrow.
                                    ↑ infinitive before modal
```

```
Er sagt, dass er schwimmen kann.        He says that he can swim.
Ich hoffe, dass du kommen kannst.       I hope that you can come.
```

## Modals in the Past

In speech, modals use Präteritum (not Perfekt — the Perfekt forms exist but sound awkward):

```
können → konnte     (could)
müssen → musste     (had to)
dürfen → durfte     (was allowed to)
sollen → sollte     (was supposed to)
wollen → wollte     (wanted to)
mögen  → mochte     (liked)
```

```
Ich konnte gestern nicht schlafen.      I couldn't sleep yesterday.
Er musste früh aufstehen.               He had to get up early.
Sie durfte nicht kommen.                She wasn't allowed to come.
Wir sollten um acht da sein.            We were supposed to be there at eight.
Er wollte nicht essen.                  He didn't want to eat.
Ich mochte ihn früher nicht.            I didn't like him before.
```

For Perfekt with modals, the structure is:
`haben` + infinitive + modal infinitive

```
Ich habe nicht kommen können.       I was not able to come.
Er hat arbeiten müssen.             He had to work.
```

This "double infinitive" construction is grammatically correct but sounds formal. In everyday speech, the Präteritum forms (`konnte`, `musste`, etc.) are simpler and more natural.

## Degrees of Obligation

Modals form a spectrum:

```
Weak ←────────────────────────────────→ Strong

möchten     wollen      sollen      müssen
would like  want        supposed to  have to / must
```

And for permission:

```
Prohibition ←─────────────────────────────→ Permission

dürfen nicht    nicht müssen    dürfen    können
must not        don't have to   may       can
```

## Common Modal Phrases

Memorize these — they cover most daily situations:

```
Kann ich bitte die Rechnung haben?      Can I have the bill please?
Darf ich hier rauchen?                  May I smoke here?
Könnte ich bitte...?                    Could I please...?    (more polite)
Dürfte ich...?                          Might I...?           (very polite)
Was soll das bedeuten?                  What is that supposed to mean?
Das muss ein Fehler sein.               That must be a mistake.
Sie müssen das nicht machen.            You don't have to do that.
Ich will das nicht.                     I don't want that.
Möchten Sie etwas trinken?              Would you like something to drink?
```

---

## können vs dürfen vs mögen for Permission

Three ways to ask permission, with different registers:

```
Kann ich das Fenster öffnen?        Can I open the window?  (casual)
Darf ich das Fenster öffnen?        May I open the window?  (polite)
Dürfte ich das Fenster öffnen?      Might I open the window? (very polite, subjunctive)
```

In most everyday situations, `kann ich` is fine. `Darf ich` is safer in formal situations. `Dürfte ich` for maximum politeness.

---

## Expressing Plans and Intentions

Modals + future time expressions cover most expressions of plans:

```
Ich will nächstes Jahr nach Japan reisen.   I want to travel to Japan next year.
Ich möchte Arzt werden.                     I would like to become a doctor.
Wir sollen um zehn ankommen.                We're supposed to arrive at ten.
Kannst du morgen kommen?                    Can you come tomorrow?
Ich muss das bis Freitag fertig machen.     I have to finish that by Friday.
```

---

## Summary

| Modal | Meaning | Key nuance |
|-------|---------|------------|
| können | can / be able to | ability, possibility |
| müssen | must / have to | necessity (internal/logical) |
| dürfen | may / be allowed | permission (external rules) |
| sollen | should / supposed to | external expectation |
| wollen | want to | desire, intention |
| mögen/möchten | like / would like | liking, polite request |

Critical: `nicht müssen` = don't have to; `nicht dürfen` = must not

Past tense: use Präteritum forms (konnte, musste, durfte, etc.)

Fill in the correct modal verb:

- Ich ___ nicht lügen. (I shouldn't lie — moral obligation)
- ___ du mir helfen? (Can you help me?)

**2. Negate correctly**

Translate — pay attention to `nicht müssen` vs `nicht dürfen`:

- You don't have to come.
- You must not come.
- We don't have to work today.
- He is not allowed to drive (= no license).

**3. Past tense of modals**

Rewrite in past tense (Präteritum):

- Ich kann nicht kommen.
- Er muss arbeiten.
- Du darfst nicht rauchen.
- Wir wollen ins Kino gehen.
- Sie soll um acht da sein.
