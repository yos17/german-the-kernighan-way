## The Short Story Model

Good short prose has a simple structure:

1. **Setting** — where, when, who
2. **Situation** — something is normal, established
3. **Disruption** — something changes or goes wrong
4. **Response** — what the person does
5. **Resolution** — how it ends

Five elements. You can write a compelling story in 10 sentences.

## A Model Story: Read First

Read this story in German. Don't reach for a dictionary on the first pass — follow the flow, pick up what you can. Then read the translation.

---

**Der verlorene Schlüssel**
*(The Lost Key)*

Es war ein gewöhnlicher Dienstagmorgen.
Anna stand um halb sieben auf, duschte und frühstückte.
Bevor sie das Haus verließ, suchte sie ihren Schlüssel — aber er war weg.

Sie durchsuchte ihre Handtasche, schaute unter dem Bett und öffnete jeden Schrank.
Nichts.

Da fiel ihr ein, dass sie gestern Abend mit dem Fahrrad gefahren war.
Sie lief schnell in den Keller, wo sie ihr Fahrrad aufbewahrte.
Der Schlüssel hing noch am Fahrradkorb.

Anna lachte, schloss die Wohnung ab und eilte zum Bahnhof.
Der Zug fuhr ab, als sie gerade die Treppe hinunterlief — aber der nächste kam in fünf Minuten.
*Könnte schlimmer sein*, dachte sie.

---

**Translation:**

It was an ordinary Tuesday morning.
Anna got up at half past six, showered and had breakfast.
Before she left the house, she looked for her key — but it was gone.

She searched her bag, looked under the bed, and opened every cupboard.
Nothing.

Then she remembered that she had ridden her bike the previous evening.
She ran quickly down to the cellar, where she kept her bike.
The key was still hanging on the bike basket.

Anna laughed, locked the apartment and hurried to the station.
The train was pulling away just as she ran down the stairs — but the next one came in five minutes.
*Could be worse*, she thought.

## Grammar in the Story

Let's annotate what's happening:

**Präteritum for narrative**: `war`, `stand auf`, `duschte`, `suchte`, `lachte`, `eilte` — all Präteritum, standard for storytelling.

**Perfekt for spoken/character thought**: `war gefahren` (Plusquamperfekt actually — she had ridden it the previous evening, further back in time).

**Subordinate clauses**: `bevor sie das Haus verließ` (bevor + verb last), `da fiel ihr ein, dass sie...` (dass + verb last), `wo sie ihr Fahrrad aufbewahrte` (relative clause with wo).

**Separable verbs**: `aufstehen → stand auf`, `aufbewahren → aufbewahrte`, `abfahren → fuhr ab`.

**Dative**: `fiel ihr ein` (ihr = dative, "it occurred to her"), `am Fahrradkorb` (an + dative).

**Two-way preposition**: `in den Keller` (accusative, direction), `im Keller` would be dative (location).

**Konjunktiv II in reported thought**: `könnte schlimmer sein` (could be worse).

## Writing Technique: Show, Don't Tell

German grammar gives you the tools. Good writing uses them to show, not announce.

**Weak**: *Er war nervös.* (He was nervous.)

**Strong**: *Seine Hände zitterten, als er das Telefon nahm.* (His hands trembled as he picked up the phone.)

**Weak**: *Sie freute sich sehr.* (She was very happy.)

**Strong**: *Sie musste lächeln, obwohl sie es versucht hatte, ernst zu bleiben.* (She had to smile, even though she had tried to stay serious.)

The grammar is the same. The specificity is different. Concrete details carry emotion better than adjectives do.

## The Grammar Checklist

Before finishing any piece of German writing, ask:

- [ ] Does every sentence have a verb in position 2? (main clauses)
- [ ] Do subordinate clauses have verb-last? (`weil`, `dass`, `wenn`, `obwohl`, etc.)
- [ ] Are article endings correct for case? (at least nominative and accusative)
- [ ] Do adjective endings match gender and case?
- [ ] Are separable verbs split correctly in main clauses?
- [ ] Are separable verbs kept together in subordinate clauses?
- [ ] Is `sein` or `haben` used correctly for Perfekt?
- [ ] Is Präteritum used for `sein`/`haben`/modals in speech?

## What Comes Next

This course covered the core grammar. Here's what to build on next:

**Reading**: Start reading German — news articles, children's books, graded readers. The grammar will click in context.

**Listening**: Podcasts for learners: *Slow German mit Annik Rubens*, *Deutsch warum nicht?* (Deutsche Welle), *Coffee Break German*.

**Writing**: Write something every day. A diary entry, a text message, a description of something you did. Output forces you to produce the grammar actively.

**Speaking**: Find a language exchange partner or tutor. Speaking forces the grammar to become automatic. Italki, Tandem, HelloTalk.

**Reference**: 
- *Hammer's German Grammar* — comprehensive reference
- *German in Review* — structured grammar progression  
- *Deutsche Welle Learn German* (free, dw.com/en/learn-german)

**The honest truth about German**: The hard parts — cases, adjective endings, verb-last in subordinate clauses — only become automatic with volume. Grammar rules get you started; exposure and practice make them instinct. Read a lot. Write a lot. Speak a lot.

The grammar is learnable. It just takes time.

## A Final Note

German grammar has a reputation for difficulty. That reputation is partly deserved — the case system, the gender system, the adjective endings, the verb-final subordinate clauses. There's genuine complexity here.

But the complexity is *systematic*. There is logic under every rule. Every case change has a reason. Every preposition triggers a specific case for a reason. The word order rules are consistent.

Once you see the system, you're not memorizing exceptions — you're working with a set of consistent rules that happened to look complicated from the outside.

You have the tools. Now use them.

*Viel Erfolg!* — Good luck!

## Summary: The Whole System

```
Nouns:          always learn with article + plural
Articles:       der/die/das → den/die/das (acc.) → dem/der/dem (dat.)
Cases:          nominative (subject), accusative (object), dative (indirect)
Verbs:          position 2 in main clause; last in subordinate clause
Past:           Perfekt (spoken), Präteritum (written, sein/haben/modals)
Adjectives:     endings depend on article + gender + case
Prepositions:   durch/für/gegen/ohne/um = acc.; aus/bei/mit/nach/seit/von/zu = dat.
Two-way preps:  movement = acc.; location = dat.
Modals:         nicht müssen ≠ not allowed to; nicht dürfen = must not
Subordinate:    verb last; weil/dass/wenn/obwohl/als/relative clauses
Konjunktiv II:  würde + inf.; wäre/hätte for sein/haben; for polite requests
```

That's German grammar. Not a mountain — a system.
