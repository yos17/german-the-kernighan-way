# Chapter 5: Word Order

*The verb-second rule. Why German sentences can start in unexpected ways.*

---

## One Rule Controls Everything

German word order seems chaotic at first. Verbs at the end. Subjects moved around. But there's one rule that governs main clauses:

**The conjugated verb must be in position 2.**

That's it. Whatever comes first — subject, time expression, place, anything — the conjugated verb is always second.

Position 1 can be *anything*. But position 2 is always the verb.

---

## Position 2, Always

A normal sentence:

```
Ich    kaufe    heute    ein Buch.
[1]    [2]      [3]      [4]
subj   verb     time     object

I buy a book today.
```

Now put "today" first:

```
Heute  kaufe    ich    ein Buch.
[1]    [2]      [3]    [4]
time   verb     subj   object

Today I buy a book.
```

The subject moved to position 3. The verb stayed in position 2. The verb never moves — everything else moves around it.

This is called **subject-verb inversion** — when something other than the subject opens the sentence, the subject and verb swap.

---

## Starting with Time

Very common in German to start with a time expression:

```
Jeden Morgen trinke ich Kaffee.         Every morning I drink coffee.
Um acht Uhr stehe ich auf.              At eight o'clock I get up.
Manchmal fahre ich mit dem Fahrrad.     Sometimes I ride my bike.
Gestern war ich sehr müde.              Yesterday I was very tired.
Nächste Woche fahren wir nach Wien.     Next week we're going to Vienna.
```

Notice: subject follows verb when something else is in position 1. This is normal German. Not an exception, not advanced — just the standard rule.

---

## Starting with a Place

```
In Berlin gibt es viele Museen.     In Berlin there are many museums.
Hier wohnt meine Schwester.         Here lives my sister.
Zu Hause esse ich immer.            At home I always eat.
```

## The Position of "Nicht"

Where does `nicht` (not) go?

**Negating the whole sentence**: `nicht` goes to the end (or before the infinitive/participle):

```
Ich kaufe das Buch nicht.           I don't buy the book.
Er kommt heute nicht.               He isn't coming today.
Ich kann nicht kommen.              I can't come.
```

**Negating a specific element**: `nicht` goes directly before that element:

```
Nicht ich habe das gemacht.         Not *I* did that (someone else did).
Ich komme nicht heute, sondern morgen.  I'm coming not today, but tomorrow.
```

General rule: `nicht` as late as possible, but before infinitives, past participles, adjectives, and separated prefixes.

---

## The TEKAMOLO Rule

Within the "rest" of the sentence (positions 3+), elements follow a preferred order:

**TE-KA-MO-LO**

- **TE**: temporal (when)
- **KA**: kausal (why / because of what)
- **MO**: modal (how)
- **LO**: lokal (where)

```
Ich fahre   [morgen]  [wegen der Arbeit]  [mit dem Zug]  [nach Berlin].
            [TE]      [KA]                [MO]           [LO]
```

Translation: I'm traveling tomorrow because of work by train to Berlin.

This is a guideline, not an iron rule. But when in doubt, TEKAMOLO gives you a natural-sounding word order.

---

## Questions

**Yes/no questions**: verb moves to position 1 (subject goes after):

```
Statement:   Du  kaufst  ein Buch.     You buy a book.
Question:    Kaufst  du  ein Buch?     Do you buy a book?
                [1]  [2]
```

```
Hast du Hunger?             Are you hungry?
Kommt er morgen?            Is he coming tomorrow?
Sprechen Sie Englisch?      Do you speak English?
```

**W-questions**: question word in position 1, verb in position 2, subject in position 3:

```
Was  kaufst  du  heute?         What are you buying today?
[1]  [2]     [3] [4]

Wann  kommt  er  zurück?        When is he coming back?
Wo    wohnt  sie?               Where does she live?
Warum  lernst  du  Deutsch?     Why are you learning German?
```

---

## Subordinate Clauses: Verb Goes Last

In subordinate clauses (introduced by conjunctions like `weil`, `dass`, `wenn`, `ob`, etc.), the verb moves to the **very end**.

```
Main clause:        Er kommt nicht.         He isn't coming.
Subordinate:   ...  weil er krank ist.  ...  because he is sick.
                                        ↑ verb at the end
```

Full sentences:

```
Ich bleibe zu Hause, weil ich krank bin.
I'm staying home because I'm sick.

Ich weiß, dass er morgen kommt.
I know that he's coming tomorrow.

Wenn es regnet, bleibe ich drin.
If it rains, I stay inside.

Er fragt, ob sie Deutsch spricht.
He asks whether she speaks German.
```

Common subordinating conjunctions:

```
weil        because
dass        that
wenn        if / when
ob          whether
obwohl      although
bevor       before
nachdem     after
während     while
bis         until
seit(dem)   since
```

**Modal verb in subordinate clause**: the modal goes last, infinitive just before it:

```
Ich weiß, dass er Deutsch sprechen kann.
I know that he can speak German.
(literally: ...that he German speak can)
```

**Separable verb in subordinate clause**: the prefix stays attached:

```
Ich weiß, dass er um acht aufsteht.
I know that he gets up at eight.
(not: ...dass er um acht steht auf)
```

---

## Coordinating vs. Subordinating

Some conjunctions don't move the verb — they just connect two main clauses. These are **coordinating conjunctions**:

```
und         and
oder        or
aber        but
denn        because (= for/since — more formal)
sondern     but rather (after a negative)
```

```
Ich lerne Deutsch und er lernt Spanisch.
I learn German and he learns Spanish.
(verb position unchanged in both halves)

Er kommt nicht, denn er ist krank.
He isn't coming, for/because he is sick.
(denn is coordinating → verb stays in position 2)
```

Contrast with `weil` (subordinating):

```
Er kommt nicht, weil er krank ist.
He isn't coming because he is sick.
(weil is subordinating → verb goes to end)
```

`denn` and `weil` both mean "because" but behave differently. In spoken German, people increasingly use `weil` for both (sometimes even with verb in position 2, informally). In written German, keep them distinct.

## Putting It Together: A News Item

Apply everything. Write a short paragraph in the style of a news brief:

```
Gestern Abend fand in Berlin ein Konzert statt.
Last night a concert took place in Berlin.

Über 5000 Menschen kamen, obwohl es stark regnete.
Over 5000 people came, although it rained heavily.

Die Sängerin sagte, dass sie sehr glücklich sei.
The singer said that she was very happy.

Nächsten Monat wird sie auch in München spielen.
Next month she will also play in Munich.
```

---

## Summary

| Situation | Verb position |
|-----------|--------------|
| Normal statement | Position 2 |
| Time/place/other first | Position 2 (subject inverts) |
| Yes/no question | Position 1 |
| W-question | Position 2 (after question word) |
| Subordinate clause | Last position |

Within the "rest" of the sentence: TE-KA-MO-LO (time, cause, manner, place)

`nicht` goes as late as possible, but before infinitives and participles.
