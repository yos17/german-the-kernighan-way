# Chapter 10: Subordinate Clauses

*Verb-last. Relative clauses. Connecting complex thoughts.*

---

## Why Subordinate Clauses Matter

Without subordinate clauses, you're writing like a child:

*"Ich bin müde. Ich habe nicht geschlafen. Das Bett war kaputt."*

With subordinate clauses, you're writing like an adult:

*"Ich bin müde, weil ich nicht schlafen konnte, da das Bett kaputt war."*

The ideas connect. Cause and effect appear. Context is built. This is the difference between functional German and fluent German.

---

## The Core Rule

In a subordinate clause, the **conjugated verb goes to the end**.

```
Main clause:   Er kommt nicht.         He isn't coming.
               [verb in position 2]

Subordinate:   ...weil er krank ist.   ...because he is sick.
               [verb at the end]
```

The subordinating conjunction (`weil`, `dass`, `wenn`, etc.) triggers this shift. It's a signal: "everything that follows has verb-last word order."

---

## Causal Clauses: weil and da

**weil** — because (gives the reason)

```
Ich bleibe zu Hause, weil ich krank bin.
I'm staying home because I'm sick.

Er kann nicht kommen, weil er arbeiten muss.
He can't come because he has to work.

Ich lerne Deutsch, weil ich in Deutschland arbeiten möchte.
I'm learning German because I want to work in Germany.
```

**da** — since / as (the reason is already known or obvious)

```
Da ich krank bin, bleibe ich zu Hause.
Since I'm sick, I'm staying home.
(the sickness is established context)
```

`weil` gives new information. `da` refers to established context. In practice, they're often interchangeable.

---

## Content Clauses: dass

**dass** — that (introduces reported speech, opinions, knowledge)

```
Ich weiß, dass er kommt.            I know that he's coming.
Ich denke, dass das richtig ist.     I think that is correct.
Sie sagt, dass sie müde ist.         She says that she's tired.
Es stimmt, dass Deutsch schwer ist.  It's true that German is hard.
```

Common verbs before `dass`:
```
wissen          to know
denken          to think
glauben         to believe
sagen           to say
hoffen          to hope
finden          to find / consider
fühlen          to feel
sehen           to see / perceive
```

Omitting `dass`: in informal German, `dass` is often dropped (like "that" in English), but the verb still goes last:

```
Ich glaube, das stimmt.     (informal — verb stays in position 2)
Ich glaube, dass das stimmt. (formal — verb at end)
```

## Conditional Clauses: wenn and als

**wenn** — when (recurring), if (conditional)

```
Wenn es regnet, bleibe ich zu Hause.
If/When it rains, I stay home.

Ich helfe dir, wenn ich Zeit habe.
I'll help you when I have time.

Wenn ich Geld hätte, würde ich reisen.
If I had money, I would travel.  (subjunctive — see later)
```

**als** — when (specific past moment, single event)

```
Als ich jung war, lebte ich in Berlin.
When I was young, I lived in Berlin.

Als er ankam, war es schon dunkel.
When he arrived, it was already dark.
```

Use `wenn` for the present, future, and recurring past events.
Use `als` for a single past moment.

```
Wenn ich nach Hause komme, esse ich immer zuerst.
When(ever) I come home, I always eat first. (recurring)

Als ich nach Hause kam, war sie nicht da.
When I came home, she wasn't there. (single past moment)
```

---

## Concessive Clauses: obwohl

**obwohl** — although, even though

```
Obwohl es regnet, gehe ich spazieren.
Although it's raining, I'm going for a walk.

Er kommt, obwohl er müde ist.
He's coming even though he's tired.

Obwohl Deutsch schwer ist, macht es Spaß.
Although German is hard, it's fun.
```

---

## Temporal Clauses

**bevor** — before

```
Bevor ich schlafe, lese ich immer.
Before I sleep, I always read.
```

**nachdem** — after

```
Nachdem ich gegessen hatte, machte ich einen Spaziergang.
After I had eaten, I went for a walk.
(note: nachdem usually triggers Plusquamperfekt — had done)
```

**während** — while

```
Während er kochte, las sie die Zeitung.
While he was cooking, she was reading the newspaper.
```

**bis** — until

```
Ich warte, bis du fertig bist.
I'll wait until you're done.
```

**seit(dem)** — since (temporal)

```
Seitdem er Deutsch lernt, hört er viele Podcasts.
Since he's been learning German, he listens to many podcasts.
```

## Relative Clauses

Relative clauses describe a noun using "who", "which", "that" in English.

```
Der Mann, der dort steht, ist mein Freund.
The man who is standing there is my friend.
```

The relative pronoun (`der`, `die`, `das`, `den`, etc.) matches the **gender and number** of the noun it refers to, but its **case** depends on its role in the relative clause.

Relative pronouns look almost exactly like definite articles, with a few differences:

| | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter | Plural |
|--|-----------|----------|--------|--------|
| Nominative | **der** | **die** | **das** | **die** |
| Accusative | **den** | **die** | **das** | **die** |
| Dative | **dem** | **der** | **dem** | **denen** |
| Genitive | **dessen** | **deren** | **dessen** | **deren** |

Differences from definite article: dative plural is `denen` (not `den`), genitive exists.

**Examples:**

The man **who** is standing there — `der Mann` is masculine, and he's the subject of the relative clause (nominative):

```
Der Mann, der dort steht, ist mein Freund.
```

The woman **whom** I know — `die Frau` is feminine, and she's the object (accusative):

```
Die Frau, die ich kenne, wohnt in Wien.
The woman whom I know lives in Vienna.
```

The child **to whom** I gave the book — `das Kind` is neuter, but its role in the relative clause is dative:

```
Das Kind, dem ich das Buch gegeben habe, heißt Max.
The child to whom I gave the book is called Max.
```

The car **that** I bought — accusative, neuter:

```
Das Auto, das ich gekauft habe, ist rot.
The car that I bought is red.
```

Key: the relative clause ends with the verb, just like any subordinate clause.

## Indirect Questions

When you embed a question inside a sentence, it becomes a subordinate clause with verb-last:

```
Direct question:   Wann kommt er?          When is he coming?
Indirect:          Ich weiß nicht, wann er kommt.  I don't know when he is coming.
                                                     (verb goes last)
```

```
Wo ist er?          →  Ich weiß nicht, wo er ist.
Was macht er?       →  Kannst du mir sagen, was er macht?
Ob er kommt?        →  Ich frage, ob er kommt.
Wer das war?        →  Ich weiß nicht, wer das war.
```

Useful frames for indirect questions:

```
Ich weiß nicht, ...             I don't know...
Können Sie mir sagen, ...?      Can you tell me...?
Ich frage mich, ...             I wonder...
Ich verstehe nicht, ...         I don't understand...
Es ist unklar, ...              It's unclear...
```

## Stacking Clauses

Complex thoughts combine multiple clause types:

```
Ich glaube, dass er nicht kommt, weil er krank ist.
I think that he isn't coming because he is sick.
```

```
Obwohl ich müde bin, gehe ich spazieren, weil das Wetter so schön ist.
Although I'm tired, I'm going for a walk because the weather is so nice.
```

When a subordinate clause itself opens the sentence, the main clause inverts (position 1 is occupied):

```
Weil ich krank bin, [main clause with inverted verb]...
Weil ich krank bin, bleibe ich zu Hause.
```

---

## Summary

| Conjunction | Type | Verb position |
|------------|------|--------------|
| weil, da | reason | end |
| dass | content | end |
| wenn | if/when (recurring) | end |
| als | when (past, single) | end |
| obwohl | although | end |
| bevor, nachdem, während, bis, seitdem | time | end |

Relative pronouns: match noun gender/number; case = role in relative clause

Indirect questions: question word/ob acts like subordinating conjunction → verb last

## Exercises

**1. Combine with weil**

Combine into one sentence using `weil`:

- Ich lerne Deutsch. Ich möchte in Wien leben.
- Er kommt nicht. Er hat keine Zeit.
- Sie ist glücklich. Sie hat eine neue Stelle.
- Wir fahren nicht. Das Auto ist kaputt.

**2. dass sentences**

Express these as `dass` sentences:

- I know: he is in Berlin.
- I think: German is interesting.
- She says: she is tired.
- I hope: you can come.

**3. wenn vs als**

Fill in `wenn` or `als`:

- ___ ich klein war, wohnte ich in Hamburg. (past, single period)
- ___ ich nach Hause komme, mache ich immer Kaffee. (recurring)
- ___ sie das hörte, wurde sie sehr traurig. (single past moment)
- ___ es regnet, nehme ich immer einen Schirm. (conditional/recurring)

**4. Relative clauses**

Build a relative clause to describe each noun. Check the gender and the role in the relative clause:

- Der Mann ___ heißt Peter. (who lives in Berlin)
- Die Frau ___ ist meine Lehrerin. (whom I know)
- Das Buch ___ ist sehr interessant. (that I am reading)
- Die Kinder ___ spielen im Garten. (to whom I gave the ball — dative)

**5. Write complex thoughts**

Write a paragraph of 6 sentences describing your reasons for learning German (or any other subject). Use at least: `weil`, `obwohl`, `dass`, and one relative clause.
