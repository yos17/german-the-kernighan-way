# Chapter 7: Adjectives

*Endings that change. The system behind them.*

---

## The Problem with German Adjectives

In English, adjectives don't change: a big house, a big car, a big dog.

In German, adjectives before a noun change based on: the gender, the case, and whether there's an article. Three variables. The result looks like a nightmare table. But there's a pattern underneath.

Adjectives after a verb don't change at all:

```
Das Haus ist groß.      The house is big.          (no ending)
Das ist ein großes Haus. That is a big house.       (ending: -es)
```

The endings only appear when the adjective is **before a noun**.

---

## The Logic: Who Carries the Gender Signal?

Here's the key insight. German sentences need to signal gender, case, and number somewhere. When there's a definite article (`der/die/das`), the article carries that signal — it already changed form.

```
Der alte Mann schläft.       The old man is sleeping.
```

`der` already tells us: masculine, nominative. The adjective doesn't need to carry that signal again. So it gets a "weak" ending: just `-e`.

When there's **no article** (or an indefinite article that doesn't fully signal gender), the adjective has to carry the signal itself. So it gets "strong" endings — endings that look like the article.

This is the logic:

- **After definite article** (der/die/das/die): adjective adds *weak* endings
- **After indefinite article** (ein/eine/ein): adjective adds *mixed* endings  
- **No article**: adjective adds *strong* endings (looks like the definite article)

---

## Weak Endings (after der/die/das)

After a definite article, most endings are just `-e` or `-en`:

| | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter | Plural |
|--|-----------|----------|--------|--------|
| Nominative | -e | -e | -e | -en |
| Accusative | -en | -e | -e | -en |
| Dative | -en | -en | -en | -en |

The pattern: nominative singular is `-e` for all genders. Everything else is `-en`.

```
der alte Mann           the old man         (nom. m. → -e)
die alte Frau           the old woman       (nom. f. → -e)
das alte Haus           the old house       (nom. n. → -e)

Ich sehe den alten Mann.            I see the old man.     (acc. m. → -en)
Ich helfe der alten Frau.           I help the old woman.  (dat. f. → -en)
Ich gehe in das alte Haus.          I enter the old house. (acc. n. → -e)
```

---

## Mixed Endings (after ein/eine/ein)

After indefinite articles, the adjective needs to fill in where the article is ambiguous.

The indefinite article `ein` doesn't distinguish masculine/neuter (both are `ein`), so the adjective compensates:

| | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter | Plural (no article) |
|--|-----------|----------|--------|---------------------|
| Nominative | -er | -e | -es | -e |
| Accusative | -en | -e | -es | -e |
| Dative | -en | -en | -en | -en |

```
ein alter Mann          an old man          (nom. m. → -er)
eine alte Frau          an old woman        (nom. f. → -e)
ein altes Haus          an old house        (nom. n. → -es)

Ich sehe einen alten Mann.          I see an old man.      (acc. m. → -en)
```

Nominative/accusative neuter gets `-es` because `ein` alone doesn't show gender there.

---

## Strong Endings (no article)

When there's no article at all, the adjective carries the full signal — its endings mirror the definite article:

| | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter | Plural |
|--|-----------|----------|--------|--------|
| Nominative | -er | -e | -es | -e |
| Accusative | -en | -e | -es | -e |
| Dative | -em | -er | -em | -en |

```
Kalter Kaffee schmeckt nicht.       Cold coffee doesn't taste good.
Frische Luft ist gesund.            Fresh air is healthy.
Gutes Essen macht glücklich.        Good food makes you happy.

Mit frischem Brot.                  With fresh bread.     (dat. n. → -em)
```

---

## In Practice: A Simpler Approach

The three tables are real, but you don't need to memorize all three at once. Here's a practical approach:

**Step 1**: Does the article already show the gender/case clearly?
- Yes → weak ending (probably `-en` or `-e`)
- No → adjective adds the signal (stronger ending with vowel from article)

**Step 2**: The most common ending is `-en`. When in doubt, use `-en`. You'll be wrong sometimes, but you'll be understood.

**Step 3**: Get nominative right first. Then accusative. Dative last.

## Comparatives and Superlatives

**Comparative** (more, -er): add `-er` to the adjective

```
klein → kleiner     (smaller)
groß → größer       (bigger — note umlaut)
alt → älter         (older)
jung → jünger       (younger)
gut → besser        (better — irregular)
viel → mehr         (more — irregular)
```

Use with `als` (than):

```
Er ist größer als ich.          He is taller than me.
Berlin ist größer als Hamburg.  Berlin is bigger than Hamburg.
Das ist besser als nichts.      That is better than nothing.
```

**Superlative** (most, -est): `am` + adjective + `-sten`

```
am kleinsten    (smallest)
am größten      (biggest)
am ältesten     (oldest)
am besten       (best)
am meisten      (most)
```

```
Das ist das beste Restaurant.       That is the best restaurant.
Er ist am fleißigsten.              He is the most hardworking.
```

## Describing People and Places

Put it all together. Describe a person:

```
Meine Schwester ist groß und schlank.
My sister is tall and slim.

Sie hat kurze blonde Haare und blaue Augen.
She has short blonde hair and blue eyes.

Sie ist eine freundliche und kluge Frau.
She is a friendly and clever woman.

Ihre Wohnung ist klein aber sehr schön.
Her apartment is small but very beautiful.
```

Describe a city:

```
Hamburg ist eine große Hafenstadt im Norden Deutschlands.
Hamburg is a large port city in northern Germany.

Die Innenstadt ist belebt und modern.
The city centre is lively and modern.

Es gibt viele alte und historische Gebäude.
There are many old and historic buildings.

Das Wetter ist oft kalt und grau, aber die Stadt ist trotzdem schön.
The weather is often cold and grey, but the city is beautiful nonetheless.
```

---

## Summary

Three adjective declension patterns:

| Context | Pattern | Key endings |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| After der/die/das | Weak | mostly -e and -en |
| After ein/eine | Mixed | -er/-es where article is ambiguous |
| No article | Strong | mirrors the article itself |

Comparative: `-er` + `als`
Superlative: `am` + `-sten`

Practical strategy: when in doubt, use `-en`. Get nominative right first.
